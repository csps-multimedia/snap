Protected Information versus Classified Information

Broad descriptions of the security categories and their associated degree of injury follow.  However, please note that there are exceptions to these, so please refer to your departmental security office to find out how they categorize information in your department.

Select the buttons to learn more about each category.

Protected Information

Information is categorized as “Protected A,” “Protected B” or “Protected C” when unauthorized disclosure could reasonably be expected to cause injury outside of the national interest.

Protected A

Applies to information that, if compromised, could reasonably be expected to cause limited or moderate injury outside the national interest, such as a minor loss of privacy or financial loss.

Examples:

  • Personal information such as name, home address, home telephone number, date of birth
  • Exact salary figure
  • Letters of offer
Protected B

Applies to information that, if compromised, could reasonably be expected to cause serious injury outside the national interest such as loss of competitive advantage, prejudicial treatment, loss of reputation or significant loss of privacy.

Examples:

  • Personal information such as medical information, finances or credit, or personnel assessments
  • Social Insurance Number when combined with other personal information
  • Business information related to trade secrets, competitive position, or contract negotiations
Protected C

Applies to the very limited amount of information that, if compromised, could reasonably be expected to cause extremely grave injury outside the national interest, such as loss of life or extremely large financial impact.

Examples:

  • Witness protection program
  • Police informants
  • Criminal intelligence

Classified Information

Information is categorized as “classified” (that is, “Confidential,” “Secret” or “Top Secret”) when unauthorized disclosure could reasonably be expected to cause injury to the national interest.

Confidential

This applies when unauthorized disclosure could reasonably be expected to cause limited or moderate injury to the national interest.

Examples:

  • Cause damage to diplomatic or federal-provincial relations (e.g. cause formal protest or other sanction)
  • Damage to national finances or economic interests
  • Disrupt significant national operations or critical national infrastructure
Secret

This applies when unauthorized disclosure could reasonably be expected to cause serious injury to the national interest.

Examples:

  • Serious damage to relations with provincial or foreign governments
  • Threaten life directly; seriously prejudice public order, safety or national security
  • Substantial material damage to national finances or economic interests
  • Shut down or substantially disrupt critical national infrastructure
Top Secret

This applies to the very limited amount of information that, if compromised could reasonably be expected to cause exceptionally grave injury to the national interest.

Examples:

  • Lead directly to widespread loss of life
  • Disrupt foreign relations vitally affecting the national security
  • Compromise vital national defense plans or sensitive intelligence operations

National Interest

This concerns the defense and maintenance of the social, political, and economic stability of Canada.
