Predominant Threats

The nature of your work, the information and assets in your workplace or that you have access to on your network, and your general work environment will dictate what your predominant threat will be.

As a public servant, you have a duty and obligation to understand the potential threats that could endanger your program and the information and assets that have been entrusted to you.

Generally, your foremost concern should be with deliberate threats trying to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information and assets.

Example 1

Government of Canada systems and networks may be targeted by a wide variety of threat actors looking to steal government information. These attackers typically gain access by targeting users with malicious e-mails that appear to be legitimate. Some network attacks are sophisticated and may remain undetected for a long period of time. How to spot malicious e-mail messages will be covered in more depth in later modules; however, the following Spotting Malicious E-mail Messages - Guidance for the Government of Canada will provide some tips.

Example 2

Government employees on international travel may be targeted by foreign adversaries. Individuals holding senior positions within government and/or those who work with valuable information may be at higher risk. Adversaries have the capability to target electronic devices or may monitor networks, hotels or business centres to obtain sensitive information. In later modules, we will discuss best practices in keeping yourself and government devices safe while travelling.

Example 3

If you work for a department or agency that handles financial information, an important threat could be criminal fraud through manipulation of financial information. If you have proprietary information, then your greatest threat might be unauthorized access by business competitors. (e.g. PSPC, Receiver General, Service Canada, any department receiving proprietary information from businesses as part of a regulatory function or scientific work, e.g. Health Canada, Agriculture Canada, Natural Resources Canada, National Research Council, etc.).

Example 4

If you work in an environment where particularly sensitive or large quantities of personal information are processed, then you should be aware of how the compromise or loss of this information could cause harm to both the department in which you work and the individuals whose information is affected (e.g. victim’s information – Correctional Service Canada). Criminal activity such as identity theft may be a primary concern, but unauthorized access to personal information (e.g. tax information of individuals) or accidental disclosure through mishandling of portable media may also be of concern (e.g. Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada, Service Canada, Passport Canada, Canada Revenue Agency, Veterans Affairs Canada).
