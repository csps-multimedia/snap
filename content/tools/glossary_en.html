Glossary

  • Access Card
    A document issued by a department showing the zone or facility to which the bearer has authorized access.
  • Access Control Methods
    The methods used to prevent unauthorized access. The methods might include person-based systems which make use of guards or receptionists, systems based on physical characteristics such as fingerprints or signatures, or systems based on access control items such as turnstiles, locked doors, magnetic cards, or proximity cards.
  • Antivirus
    Antivirus (AV) software is specialized security software that detect known forms of malware. They are not limited to viruses, but may be ineffective against some forms such as rootkits.
  • Assets
    Tangible or intangible things of the Government of Canada. Assets include but are not limited to information in all forms and media, networks, systems, materiel, real property, financial resources, employee trust, public confidence and international reputation.
  • ATIP
    Access to Information Act gives Canadian citizens the right to access information in federal government records. The Act is closely related to security because it provides the legislative basis for the release, or exemptions from release, of government information.
  • Attack
    Any action to execute a threat.
  • Authorized users
    Include employees of the federal government, as well as contractors and other individuals who have been authorized to access electronic networks.
  • Availability
    The state of being accessible and usable in a timely and reliable manner.
  • Breach
    A security breach is an act or omission, deliberate or accidental that results in an actual or possible compromise.
  • Business Continuity Planning
    The development and timely execution of plans, measures, procedures and arrangements to ensure minimal or no interruption to the availability of critical services and assets.
  • Classified Assets
    Assets whose compromise would reasonably be expected to cause injury to the national interest.
  • Classified Information
    Information related to the national interest that may qualify for an exemption or exclusion under the Access to Information Act or Privacy Act, and the compromise of which would reasonably be expected to cause injury to the national interest.
  • CLC
    Canada Labour Code – Part II of the Canada Labour Code on occupational health and safety provides guidance to the federal government to prevent accidents and injury. This includes measures to protect employees (e.g. prevention of violence in the workplace).
  • Compromise
    The unauthorized access to, disclosure, destruction, removal, modification, use or interruption of assets or information.
  • COMSEC
    Communication Security - The application of cryptographic security, transmission and emission security, physical security measures, operational practices and controls to deny unauthorized access to information derived from telecommunications and that ensure the authenticity of such telecommunications.
  • Confidential
    The classification marking used when unauthorized disclosure, destruction, removal, modification or interruption of the information could reasonably be expected to cause injury to the National interest.
  • Confidentiality
    A characteristic applied to information to signify that it can only be disclosed to authorized individuals to prevent injury to national or other interests.
  • Control of Access
    Ensuring authorized access to assets within a facility or restricted areas by screening visitors and material at entry points by personnel, guards or automated means and, where required, monitoring their movement within the facility or restricted access areas by escorting them.
  • Critical Service
    A service whose compromise in terms of availability or integrity would result in a high degree of injury to the health, safety, security or economic well-being of Canadians or to the effective functioning of the Government of Canada (GC).
  • Cyber Attacks
    A cyber attack is deliberate exploitation of computer systems and networks. Cyber attacks use various means of malicious acts that either steals, alters, or destroys information.
  • Department
    All departments named in Schedule I, divisions or branches of the federal public administration set out in column I of Schedule I.1, corporations named in Schedule II, and portions of the federal public administration named in schedules IV and V of the Financial Administration Act (FAA), unless excluded by specific acts, regulations or Orders in Council.
  • Designated Organization Screening
    An administrative determination that an organization is eligible, from a security viewpoint, to access Protected information and assets.
  • Destruction
    The obliteration of information or assets. Accidental destruction of assets can occur as a result of fire, flood, earthquake or similar calamity, but may also be caused by negligence or a deliberate action such as vandalism, riot, sabotage or acts of war.
  • Detection
    The use of appropriate devices, systems and procedures to signal that an attempted or actual unauthorized access has occurred.
  • Devices
    Tools used to gather, process, receive, display, transmit, reconfigure, scan, store or destroy information electronically.
  • Disclosure
    The release of information, by any method, to any person inside or outside the department that controls the information, who is not authorized to obtain access to the information.
  • Doubled Sealed Envelope
    A sealed envelope (inner envelope) appropriately addressed and having security markings, enclosed with another sealed envelope (outer envelope).
  • DSO
    Departmental Security Officer
  • Dumpster diving
    Searching for invoices or other documents containing sensitive information in the trash.
  • Electronic Networks
    Groups of computers and computer systems that can communicate with each other. Without restricting the generality of the foregoing, these networks include the Internet, networks internal to a department, and public and private networks external to a department.
  • EMA
    Emergency Management Act – sets out how to prepare, prevent, mitigate and recover when faced with national emergency situations and disruption of critical services that impact the health, safety, security and economic well-being of Canadians. Security plays an essential role in the establishment of business continuity plans.
  • Employee
    An individual employed by a government department or agency on an indeterminate, term, secondment or casual basis.
  • Encryption
    The process of encoding messages or information in such a way that only authorized individuals can read it.
  • Facility
    A physical setting used to serve a specific purpose. A facility may be part of a building, a whole building, or a building plus its site; or it may be a construction that is not a building. The term encompasses both the physical object and its use (for example, weapons ranges, agriculture fields).
  • Facility Security Clearance
    An administrative determination that an organization is eligible, from a security viewpoint to access Classified information and assets.
  • Firewalls
    Firewall is specialized security software that blocks or restricts access to a computer or network.
  • For Cause
    A determination that there is sufficient reason to review, revoke, suspend or downgrade a reliability status, a security clearance or site access.
  • Foreign State
    Means any state other than Canada
  • Government Information
    information created, received, used, and maintained regardless of physical form, and information prepared for or produced by the Government of Canada and deemed to be under its control in the conduct of government activities or in pursuance of legal obligations.
  • Hacker
    Person who uses programming competence and knowledge of systems to gain unauthorized access to a computer or a network.
  • Hacking
    Hacking is a term used to describe actions taken by someone to gain unauthorized access to a computer. The availability of information online on the tools, techniques, and malware makes it easier for even non-technical people to undertake malicious activities.
  • Harassment
    Any behaviour that demeans, embarrasses, humiliates, annoys, alarms or verbally abuses a person and that is known or would be expected to be unwelcome. This includes words, gestures, intimidation, bullying, or other inappropriate activities.
  • High Security Zone
    An area that can only be accessed by authorized personnel or properly-escorted visitors who possess both appropriate security screening or clearance and a need to know. Top-Secret and Protected C information is typically processed in this type of zone. The area is monitored continuously (i.e. 24 hours a day, 7 days a week) and details of access are recorded and audited.
  • Identification Card
    Document issued by the organization to identify the bearer.
  • Individuals
    Casuals, indeterminate employees, students and contract workers
  • Information Management
    A discipline that directs and supports effective and efficient management of information in an organization, from planning and systems development to disposal or long-term preservation.
  • Information Technology
    Includes any equipment or system that is used in the automatic acquisition, storage, manipulation, management, movement, control, display, switching, interchange, transmission, or reception of data or information. It includes all matters concerned with the design, development, installation and implementation of information systems and applications to meet business requirements.
  • Integrity
    The state of being accurate, complete, authentic and intact.
  • Keystroke Loggers
    Keystroke loggers is malware that records the keys struck on a keyboard in a covert manner so that the person using the keyboard is unaware that their actions are being monitored.
  • Malicious Code
    An element intended to design viruses, trojan horses, worms and generally any program or code destined to create computer problems as opposed to solving them.
  • Malware
    Malicious software that infects your computer, such as computer viruses, worms, Trojan horses, spyware, and adware.
  • Material
    Any tangible object with the exclusion of those embodying information.
  • Modification
    The corruption or alteration of information, data, software or information systems equipment. Serious often because it is difficult to ascertain that corruption or alteration has taken place, especially in the machine readable environment.
  • Monitored
    To watch for or detect a breach of security.
  • Monitored Continuously
    To confirm on a continuous basis that there has not been a breach of security. Examples include electronic intrusion detection system, or someone guarding a particular point on a constant basis.
  • Monitored Periodically
    To confirm on a regular basis that there has not been a breach of security. The frequency and diligence of monitoring is based on the recommendations of a Threat and Risk Assessment. Examples include a guard patrol, or employees working at the location.
  • National Interest
    The security and the social, political and economic stability of Canada.
  • Need to know
    The need for someone to access and know information in order to perform his or her duties.
  • Network
    A computer network can be local, or extended. It allows transmission in digital format of all types of data, which can be used by the entire network.
  • Official Records
    Are records with business value that provide evidence of the conduct of government business and decision-making such as planning documents, meeting documents, financial records, etc.
  • Operations Zone
    An area that is limited to personnel who work there and properly-escorted visitors. This area should be monitored periodically. Protected B information can be handled within an operational zone but must be locked in an approved cabinet. Additional safeguards are required if Protected C, Secret, and Top-Secret information is handled in an Operations Zone: it must be locked in an approved cabinet or safe at all times.
  • Password
    Combination of characters used in combination with a user code to access a workstation, an application, on on-line service or a network server.
  • Personal Information
    Any information about an identifiable individual other than name, title, work-related address, email and telephone number of an employee.
  • Personnel Security Clearance
    The process to ensure that those who have access to government information, assets and services are honest, trustworthy, reliable and loyal to Canada.
  • Personnel Security Screening
    The process of examining the trustworthiness and suitability of employees and, where national interest is concerned, their loyalty and associated reliability. When satisfactory, an employee is granted reliability status or a security clearance. Reliability status applies when only protected assets are concerned. When the employee has access to classified assets, a security clearance corresponding to the level of classified assets is issued. A security clearance includes reliability status.
  • Pharming
    Pharming is a common type of online fraud. A means to point you to a malicious and illegitimate website by redirecting the legitimate URL. Even if the URL is entered correctly, it can still be redirected to a fake website.
  • Phishing
    Phishing refers to email scams run by cyber criminals posing as banks and other businesses. Their aim is to trick you into revealing personal and financial information and or passwords via fake websites by clicking on embedded links or opening a file. They can compromise computers or trick you out of money.
  • Physical Security
    The use of physical safeguards to prevent and delay unauthorized access to assets, detect attempted and actual unauthorized access, and activate an appropriate response.
  • Piggybacking
    Piggybacking refers to when an individual tags along or follows an authorized employee through a security checkpoint or doorway without being processed through the system. The act is unacceptable and may be legal or illegal, authorized or unauthorized, depending on the circumstances.
  • Privacy Act
    Privacy Act – protects the privacy of individuals by setting requirements for collection, use, retention or disposal of personal information in the federal government. Security plays an essential role in the protection of personal information from unauthorized disclosure or use.
  • Prohibited usage
    May include criminal offences, contraventions of non criminal regulatory federal and provincial statues, and actions that make an authorized individual or an institution liable to a civil lawsuit. Also, these activities may expose the department's network to malicious attack and exploitation.
  • Protected
    The category which indicates that the information qualifies as other sensitive information and requires enhanced protection.
  • Protected A
    Low-sensitive information. Its compromise could reasonably be expected to cause limited injury outside the national interest, such as a minor loss of privacy or financial loss.
  • Protected Assets
    Assets, other than information, whose unauthorized disclosure would reasonably be expected to cause injury to a non-national interest.
  • Protected B
    Particularly sensitive information. Compromise could reasonably be expected to cause serious injury outside the national interest such as loss of competitive advantage, prejudicial treatment, loss of reputation or significant loss of privacy.
  • Protected C
    Extremely sensitive information. Compromised, could reasonably be expected to cause grave injury outside the national interest, such as loss of life or extremely large financial impact.
  • Protected Information
    Information related to other than the national interest that may qualify for an exemption or exclusion under the Access to Information Act or Privacy Act, and the compromise of which would reasonably be expected to cause injury to a non-national interest.
  • Protection
    For physical security, protection means the use of physical, procedural and psychological barriers to delay or deter unauthorized access, including visual and acoustic barriers.
  • Public Zone
    The area surrounding a government facility to which the general public has access. You should never handle sensitive information in public zones.
  • Reception Zone
    The transitional area from a public zone to an operations zone. Generally located at the entrance of a facility, this zone offers the first physical security challenge to the public by employing security measures such as doors and other physical barriers (turnstiles). Departmental assets or information must never be stored or left unattended in this type of zone.
  • Record
    For the purpose of this policy, records are information created, received, and maintained by an organization or person for business purposes, legal obligations, or both, regardless of medium or form.
  • Reliability Status
    Indicates the successful completion of reliability checks; allows regular access to government assets and with a need to know to PROTECTED information.
  • Removable Media Storage Devices
    The storage of media is designed to be removed from the computer with powering off the computer. Some types of removable media are designed to be read by readers and drives such as optical disks (CD's, DVD's) or memory cards. Removable media may also refer to some removable storage devices, when they are used to transport or store data, such as USB flash drives or external hard disk drives.
  • Response
    The implementation of measures to ensure that security incidents are reported to appropriate security officials and immediate and long-term corrective action taken.
  • Restricted Access Area
    Work areas where access is limited to authorized individuals includes Operations, Security and High Security Zones.
  • Risk
    The uncertainty that can create exposure to undesired future events and outcomes. It is the expression of the likelihood and impact of an event with the potential to impede the achievement of an organization's objectives.
  • Risk Assessment
    An evaluation of the chance of vulnerabilities being exploited based on the effectiveness of existing or proposed security measures.
  • Risk Management
    A systematic approach to setting the best course of action under uncertainty by identifying, assessing, understanding, acting on and communicating risk issues.
  • Rootkit
    A rootkit is a stealthy type of malware designed to hide the existence of certain processes or programs from normal methods of detection and enable continued privileged access to a computer.
  • Screening
    The process of verifying visitors and/or material at entry points of a facility or a restricted area for authorizing access.
  • Security Clearance
    Indicates successful completion of a security assessment; with a need to know, allows access to classified information. There are three Security Clearance levels: Confidential, Secret and Top Secret.
  • Security Container
    Any totally enclosed storage place for a classified asset, designed to resist force and surreptitious attacks; e.g., a safe, security cabinet, strongbox, permanent vault, demountable vault or secure room.
  • Security Control
    An administrative, operational, technical, physical or legal measure for managing security risk. This term is synonymous with safeguard.
  • Security Incident
    Any workplace violence toward an employee or any act, event or omission that could result in the compromise of information, assets or services.
  • Security Screening
    Any measure resulting in a high level of assurance that an individual can be granted specific access privileges within the context of the federal government.
  • Security Zone
    An area where access is limited to authorized personnel and authorized and properly-escorted visitors only. Protected C, Secret and Top-Secret information can be handled within a security zone but it must be locked in an approved cabinet or safe; must be monitored 24/7.
  • Sensitive Information
    Information which is confidential or of high-value, and which if compromised could result in serious consequences.
  • Shoulder Surfing
    Using direct observation techniques, such as looking over someone's shoulder, to get information. Shoulder surfing can be used to obtain a wide variety of confidential information. Be aware of your surroundings, when working on or viewing Government information in public spaces.
  • Shredding
    A mechanical cutting or grinding method of reducing standard weighs of office paper, microfilm and microfiche to fragments.
  • Social Engineering
    Social Engineering is the practice of trying to trick or manipulate people into breaking normal security procedures.
  • SOIA
    Security of Information Act identifies penalties for conduct related to information security, such as espionage, that is or is likely to be harmful to Canada.
  • SPAM
    Inappropriately using electronic messaging to send unwanted or unsolicited messages to numerous people with the hopes of enticing them to click on a link or open an attachment. Spam can also distribute malware to potential victims.
  • Telework Place
    The alternative location where the employee is permitted to carry out the work otherwise performed at or from their designated workplace.
  • Teleworking
    A flexible work arrangement whereby employees have approval to carry out some or all of their work duties from a telework place.
  • Threat
    A threat is any potential event or act that could cause one or more of the following to occur: harm, injury, unauthorized disclosure, destruction, removal, modification or interruption of sensitive information, assets or services.
  • Threat Assessment
    An evaluation of the nature, likelihood and consequences of acts or events that could place Protected or Classified information and assets at risk.
  • Top Secret
    Is the classification marking used when unauthorized disclosure, destruction, removal, modification or interruption of the information could reasonably be expected to cause exceptionally grave injury to the National interest.
  • Transitory Records
    Are those information resources that are required only for a limited time and will have no further value beyond their immediate action, such as copies for reference, FYI information or emails, personal documents, etc
  • Transmittal of Sensitive Information
    Is the transfer of sensitive information and assets from one person or place to another by someone without a need to know/access to the information/asset (third-party).
  • Transport of Sensitive Information
    Is the transfer of sensitive information and assets from one person or place to another by someone with the appropriate screening level and the need to know or access to the information/asset.
  • Trojan Horse
    A trojan horse is a type of malware that is disguised within a legitimate program while appearing to perform a desirable function. They tend to be invisible to average users, but often include a backdoor allowing unauthorized access to the target's computer to steal information or harm their host computer systems.
  • Unauthorized access
    Access to assets by an individual who is not properly security screened and/or does not have a need to know.
  • Unauthorized disclosure
    Disclosure that is forbidden by law or by governmental or departmental policies.
  • Violation
    A security violation is an act or omission, deliberate or accidental that does not result in an actual or possible compromise. A security violation does not result in the compromise of government information or assets, but it does increase the risk.
  • Virus
    A computer virus is a type of malware that, when executed, replicates itself into other computer programs or data files, and performs some type of harmful activity on infected hosts.
  • Visitor
    An individual whom does not work for the government, but has a legitimate reason for being on the premises and a need to be processed by the control of access system in place.
  • Vulnerability
    An inadequacy related to security that could increase susceptibility to compromise or injury.
  • Web 2.0 tools
    Includes internet-based tools and services that allow for participatory multi-way information sharing, dialogue, syndication, and user-generated content. This can include social media and collaborative technologies.
  • Workplace Violence
    An action, conduct, threat or gesture that can reasonably be expected to cause harm, injury or illness to an employee in the workplace.
  • Worm
    A computer worm is a standalone malware computer program that replicates itself in order to spread to other computers. Unlike a computer virus, it does not need to attach itself to an existing program. Worms almost always cause at least some harm to the network.
  • Zones
    A series of clearly discernible spaces to progressively control access.
