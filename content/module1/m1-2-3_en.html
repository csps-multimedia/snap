1 - Establish the Mandate and Budget

1 - Establish the Mandate and Budget

Defining its mandate and establishing a budget are vital to an organization’s operations. Mandates are found in the acts, regulations and charters that govern an organization. The budget presents forecast revenues and expenditures (and full-time equivalents [FTEs]) that show how the organization will perform financially if certain strategies, events and plans are implemented.

2 - Develop the Organizational Structure

2 - Develop the Organizational Structure

Roles and responsibilities are divided among positions that are related by an organizational structure. An organizational chart is usually used to illustrate the structure. To be successful, managers must implement a structure that will allow employees to contribute to the unit’s objectives. Although managers are responsible for organizational design, they are encouraged to consult an experienced HR advisor who can provide valuable advice on the various possible organizational structures and their implications for the classification of existing and future positions.

3 - Define Jobs

3 - Define Jobs

Once all available information about a given job has been collected, it is summarized in a job description that is usually prepared by the manager. There are two types of job descriptions:

  • The unique job description, associated with a single position;
  • The standardized job description, associated with several positions classified at the same occupational group and level.

Once the job description content is properly linked to the organizational structure, the organization can more easily meet its mandate. The use of standardized job descriptions is supported by the Treasury Board of Canada Secretariat as an effective management practice and must be used wherever possible.

4 - Classify Jobs and Positions

4 - Classify Jobs and Positions

The purpose of classification is to establish or confirm the occupational group, sub-group (if applicable), and level assigned to a job or position. To this end, an employee with classification authority (or a classification evaluation committee) analyzes and evaluates the work assigned to a position with the help of tools such as:

  • The definitions of the occupational group and sub-group;
  • The relevant job evaluation standard;
  • The relativity analysis results;
  • The job description; and
  • The organizational structure and context.
5 - Financial Component

5 - Financial Component

The group and level (classification) of a position is matched to the corresponding salary and benefits for that position. The processes pertaining to the establishment of organizational structures, the development of job descriptions, and the classification of positions thus have a financial impact.

An important consideration:

A classification decision can have a financial impact on:

  • Short- and long-term salary costs;
  • One or more positions within one or more organizations; and
  • The ability to achieve the expected results within the allocated salary budget for a team, a branch or an organization.

Expectations

Managers and accredited HR advisors are expected to demonstrate the same attention and knowledge when making OC decisions as is required for any other financial transaction. It is their responsibility to ensure that public funds are used wisely.

Did you know?

A classification decision has repercussions on an organization’s salary envelope.
