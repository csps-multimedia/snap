E-mails

Our increasing dependence on electronic forms of communication carries with it the increased risk posed by accidental disclosure of sensitive information. E-mail is also easily intercepted and often exploited to attack our systems and networks.

E-mail and e-mail with file attachments are among the most common sources of malicious code. The malicious software code is often embedded in common attachments like PDFs and other electronic documents, as well as Web links.

It is crucial that all employees maintain a high level of cyber awareness and scrutinize all e-mails before opening them. If you receive a suspicious e-mail, do not reply, do not click on any links, and do not open the attachments. Instead, copy down the relevant details (e-mail address, subject line, time received, etc.) and report the incident to your help desk or IT security staff as soon as possible.

Indicators of Malicious Emails

Some indicators of malicious e-mail are:

  • an unknown sender with an unfamiliar e-mail address
  • tone, language, grammar that is inconsistent with what is expected
  • a web address or attachment that does not appear relevant to the content of the e-mail
  • use of private e-mail addresses (e.g. Yahoo.ca, gmail.com, hotmail.com) that impersonate government officials or people you know
  • unsolicited bulk e-mail from an unknown sender
  • a sense of urgency or trying to create an emotional response
  • e-mails informing you that “there is a problem with your account”
  • e-mails requesting that you “click on this link” or “open this attachment”
  • phony security alerts, pop-ups or notices that warn “your computer is at risk of being infected,” typically with a link to click
  • requests for funds or account information
  • Hover over (do not click) any embedded hyperlinks to confirm the validity of the address, please consult your help desk for further guidance.

Examples of Email Attacks

A number of confirmed cyber-attacks in the government are so-called “spear phishing” e-mails. These are malicious e-mails that are addressed to a specific user and appear to be legitimate because the subject line is relevant, the message is pertinent, and/or the sender is known. You have to be very careful because it is harder to detect that these e-mails contain malicious attachments or links.

Make sure you really know who is sending the e-mail, that the tone is consistent for the sender, and that the content is really relevant to your work.

“Spam” refers to unsolicited bulk e-mails sent with the hopes of enticing recipients to buy something or provide sensitive information. Never click on a link or open an attachment. To safeguard against spam, protect your e-mail address: do NOT post your government e-mail address online or use it when filling out electronic forms for non-government matters, as per your department’s acceptable use guidelines.
