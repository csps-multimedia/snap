Acceptable and Unacceptable Use Examples

Acceptable use

Appendices B and C of the Policy on Acceptable Networks and Device Use provide lists of examples of acceptable and unacceptable use that may be permitted on government electronic networks and devices. All departments or agencies will have their own policies on acceptable usage and can restrict access to certain information based on the mandate of the department.

Work-related and Professional Development Activities

  • Conducting consultations and collaborating within the federal government
  • Tweeting, re-tweeting or sharing links to professional activities and events
  • Discussing and participating in professional issues via online forums or training activities
  • Making arrangements for work-related travel
  • Completing an online job application or participating in an online evaluation or testing

Limited Personal Use

Occasional personal use of the Internet is permitted, provided that it is conducted on personal time, is not done for financial gain, does not incur any additional costs for the department, and does not interfere with the conduct of business.

Examples include:

  • Keeping up-to-date with news and current events
  • Getting directions for a trip or searching for addresses and contact information
  • Making personal travel arrangements
  • Posting or reading reviews of products or services or making online purchases
  • Checking the weather forecast
  • Confirming bus schedule information

Paying bills or conducting personal banking online

Unacceptable use

Unacceptable use includes any activity or behaviour that:

  • may give rise to criminal offences (e.g. hacking, defamation, harassment)
  • violates federal and provincial statutes (e.g. disclosing sensitive information)
  • violates TBS or departmental policies and codes of conduct
  • impacts negatively on the performance of Government of Canada electronic networks and devices (e.g. downloading large volumes of data, unauthorized removal or installation of hardware or software)
  • impedes departmental operations or the delivery of services
  • breaches the Duty of Loyalty requirement for public servants (i.e. does not refrain from public criticism of the Government of Canada)
  • could reasonably be deemed to result in civil lawsuits or civil liability
  • is done for financial gain
