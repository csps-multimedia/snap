Safety and Security on Travel Status

This section is intended to raise awareness of potential risks associated with business travel abroad or within Canada. It proposes measures to mitigate risks to you, as a government official, and to the information or assets of the Government of Canada.

Employees should know how to prepare for business travel, and be aware of any restrictions that might be in place when travelling abroad.

You must also know how to protect government information while on travel status and be familiar with information protection guidelines pertaining to foreign travel. A pre-travel briefing can provide guidance on these topics, as well as how to react in situations of threat or in the event of an emergency during travel. Contact your manager or departmental security office for more information on a pre-travel briefing.

The following job aid provides a brief overview of some recommended Safeguards and Best Practices before, during and after travel.

Safeguards and Best Practices

Security tips Before, During and After any work-related Travel
Before

Travelers should:

  • contact their departmental IT and security resources to:
    • book a travel briefing and obtain a special or diplomatic passport, where available
    • appropriately prepare any portable devices
  • register with Global Affairs Canada (DGAC) and familiarize themselves with the latest travel advisories, which include:
    • countries’ laws and customs
    • entry and exit requirements (e.g. a fee for leaving the country)
    • health advisories/information and vaccine requirements
    • risks associated with commuting within the country
    • so much more
      • inquire on airport security and border wait times
      • prepare an itinerary and make copies of travel documents (i.e. passport/visa) and leave them with family, friends and/or colleagues
        Helpful tip: Travellers can email a copy of their documents to their personal email for easy access
      • have a “travel plan B” if, for any reason, the initial travel arrangements do not work out or for a hasty departure from the country, in the event that the security situation deteriorates
      • be mindful of sharing destination and travel plans on social media
During

Travelers should:

  • know that foreign custom agents have the authority to ask for passwords to access electronic devices, at the risk of seizing it if traveller refuses
  • never lose sight of bags and personal belongings; assume luggage will be searched
  • never identify bags with business cards; keep occupation and nationality discreet
  • continuously monitor local news channels and newspapers to assess the threat status in the local area and travel destinations
  • always be mindful of elicitation techniques used by foreign agency officers and their agents
  • always ensure travel documents, government information and assets are kept in a secure location
    • Never assume that hotel safes are secure: banks can provide a better alternative
    • Assume that everything you communicate can be intercepted. Remember that even garbage can be information
    • Never plug a portable storage device (that was given to you) into your computer, and never plug your own portable storage device into a foreign computer
  • whenever possible, refuse gifts: if the traveler must accept it, they should advise the Departmental Security Office immediately for guidance
After

Travelers should:

  • contact their departmental IT and Security resources to:
    • book a travel de-briefing, if necessary (e.g. report a possible elicitation attempt)
    • return special or diplomatic passport
    • return any loaned portable devices
  • Any government issued devices that were brought on the trip should be thoroughly screened/scrubbed before reconnecting to any network.

Tips

  • Should you ever feel unsafe, contact the embassy or consulate, as well as your organization.
  • Thoroughly research your hotel accommodations before booking. Always try to book with a reputable hotel.
  • Hotel key cards may contain a lot of your personal information. Report the loss of a hotel key to the hotel immediately. Continued vigilance is recommended to protect yourself against identity theft after your return: monitor your credit rating.

You may want to use similar security practices when travelling for leisure purposes.
