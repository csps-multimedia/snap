Security Screening Process

The purpose of security screening is to ensure that those who have access to government information, assets and services are honest, trustworthy, reliable and loyal to Canada.

In order to perform an effective security screening, it is important to adhere to the following process.

Step 1

Types and Levels of Security Screening

Reliability Status:

The minimum type of screening required when the duties or tasks of a position or contract necessitate access to protected information or assets, regardless of the duration of an assignment, appointment or contract.

An individual granted reliability status may access protected information when the duties of the position require such access (i.e. there is a “need to know”).

Reliability status is required for individuals employed within the federal government.

Security Clearance:

The type of screening required when the duties or tasks of a position or contract necessitate access to classified information or assets.

An individual granted a security clearance may access classified information when the duties of the position require such access (i.e. there is a “need to know”).

The level of classified information which may be accessed is determined by the level of security clearance granted.

Site Access Screening:

This type of screening is conducted for other individuals external to government who do not require access to sensitive information but who do require access to restricted or controlled government facilities or areas or within those facilities.

Note:

Access to either protected or classified information is only on a “need to know” basis, which means when your job requires you to have access to this information.

Individuals requiring access to classified information or assets must obtain a reliability status prior to obtaining a security clearance.

Step 2

Identify the Screening Checks to Be Conducted

A Security Screening Application and Consent Form must be completed prior to conducting a reliability check or security screening assessment.

It is imperative that written consent be given by the individual agreeing to the necessary verifications of his personal information before the screening process begins.

Check Job Aid “Checks involved in a Reliability Status and a Security Clearance” to find out more about this topic.

Step 3

Conduct a Security Briefing

As the final step in the security screening process, individuals are required to sign the Security Screening Certificate and Briefing Form.

This form advises that individuals who fail to safeguard information, release information without authority or use information for unauthorized purposes, may be in contravention of the Security of Information Act, the Access to Information Act, the Privacy Act or in breach of the Policy on Government Security.

The signing of this form must be accompanied by a briefing by a manager or other designated official outlining the general security expectations of their position.

Key Point

By signing this form, individuals acknowledge that they have been granted a security screening level and formally agree to abide by the security policy and to apply security safeguards identified in various policies and standards.

Note:

Any person with a security clearance who, intentionally and without lawful authority, communicates or agrees to communicate sensitive information, may be liable to imprisonment under the Security of Information Act. This includes communication with foreign entities or terrorist groups, as well as economic espionage.

Step 4

Updates and Changes to Position Requirements

Like your language tests, a security screening needs to be updated. The security office will advise you when they are due for renewal. You are also encouraged to be aware of when your security screening is due for renewal.

A change in position may require an individual to undergo a review and update of the security screening level based on duties and access to sensitive information.

Examples of protected and classified information

Protected Information:
  • Protected A = Addresses; Age; Race; Date of birth; and Unique identifiers such as social insurance numbers, PRI, etc.
  • Protected B = Medical information; Personal or personnel assessments; and Information received in confidence from other government organizations such as medical records, etc.
  • Protected C = Police informants; Witness protection program; Identity of undercover police officer; and Criminal intelligence.
Classified Information:
  • Confidential = Federal-provincial affairs, International affairs, Defence, and Economic interests of Canada such as trade secret or financial, commercial, scientific or technical information that belongs to the Government of Canada.
  • Secret = Plans for defence of areas and installations; Particulars of federal budget before release; Cabinet documents, including Memoranda to Cabinet, draft bills; and documents prepared for, or as a result of, Cabinet committee meetings.
  • Top Secret = Vital intelligence matters; Detailed proposed alliances; and Information relating to widespread loss of life.

Checks involved in a Reliability Status and a Security Clearance

Checks involved in a Reliability Status and a Security Clearance

A reliability status usually involves the following:

  • Verification of identity and background
  • Verification of educational and professional credentials
  • Personal and professional references
  • Financial inquiry (credit check)
  • Law enforcement inquiry (criminal record check)

The elements of a security clearance vary, depending on the clearance level being considered and could involve the following:

  • Reliability status
  • Canadian Security Intelligence Service (CSIS) security assessment

These are the baseline requirements, except as indicated (if required).
